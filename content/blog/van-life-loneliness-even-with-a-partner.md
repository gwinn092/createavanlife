When people find out we've been living in a van full-time for 8 years — both of us, together — the most common response is something about how amazing that must be. The freedom. The adventure. Never being stuck somewhere boring.

All of that is true. It's also incomplete.

There's a kind of loneliness in van life that surprises almost everyone who tries it long-term. It surprised us, even though we had each other. This is the part that doesn't make it into the highlight reels.

## The Community Problem

In a house, in a neighborhood, you have a social context that persists. Neighbors you recognize. A coffee shop where the staff knows your order. A gym where you see the same faces. A friend group that doesn't require scheduling a week in advance to see.

Van life removes all of that — not in a dramatic way, but in a quiet, cumulative way that you feel most when you've been stationary for a while in a new place and realize you don't know anyone within a hundred miles.

Moving constantly means constantly rebuilding social context from scratch. You meet people at campgrounds, co-working spaces, climbing crags, wherever. Some of those connections are meaningful and some of them last. But most of them are temporary by design — you'll both move on, and the relationship exists in the frame of that location.

After enough time, you get good at making those temporary connections. You also feel, sometimes, the weight of how few of them persist.

## Having a Partner Helps — Partially

Living in the van with my girlfriend means I am genuinely never alone in the physical sense. She's there. We talk, we make decisions together, we share the experience in real time. That's not nothing — it's a lot, actually, and it makes the hard parts significantly more manageable.

But a partner doesn't replace community. Two people can be lonely together in a way that's hard to explain to people who haven't experienced it. You can both feel the absence of the larger social world while being fully present with each other. Those two things coexist.

There are also moments when the intensity of a small space — a cargo van, no matter how well converted, is a small space — makes the loneliness feel more acute rather than less. You're with each other constantly, which is wonderful and sometimes exhausting, and the absence of other people to talk to or spend time with is felt more clearly.

## What We've Figured Out

**The internet is not a substitute, but it helps.** We stay connected with friends and family through video calls, messages, and social media in a way that wasn't available to earlier generations of nomads. It doesn't replace in-person community, but it maintains relationships that would otherwise fade faster.

**Returning to places is underrated.** The van life mythology emphasizes constant movement. In practice, returning to places — spending two months in San Diego instead of one, coming back to the same campground, going to the same climbing area for a second season — builds the accumulated familiarity that creates something closer to community. You start recognizing people. People recognize you. The temporal depth that makes social connection feel real starts to form.

**You get better at it over time.** Eight years in, neither of us finds the community aspect as hard as we did in year two. Pattern recognition kicks in: where to find people who are living similarly, how to distinguish temporary connections from ones worth investing in, how to be okay with the temporary ones without expecting more.

**Lean into the freedom during the hard moments.** The loneliness and the freedom are two sides of the same coin. When the social isolation is sharpest, we often do something that's only possible because we live this way — drive somewhere new, spend a night somewhere we couldn't get to in a day trip, change the scene entirely. The thing that sometimes causes the problem is also the thing that most reliably solves it.

## Why We're Still Doing This

Eight years is long enough that if van life were mostly hard, we'd have stopped. It isn't mostly hard. Most days are genuinely good in a way that house life, for us, wasn't.

The freedom is real. The beauty of waking up somewhere new is real. The simplicity of a life that doesn't require a mortgage, a lease, or accumulating stuff — all of it is real.

The loneliness is also real. Pretending otherwise doesn't serve anyone who's thinking about making this change. It's part of what you're signing up for.

But it's manageable. It's navigable. And for us, after 8 years, it's clearly worth it.
