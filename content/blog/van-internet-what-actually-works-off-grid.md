Reliable van internet comes from layered redundancy, not one magic device.

## Baseline Setup

The core problem with rural connectivity is that no single carrier covers everywhere. Verizon dominates in some regions, T-Mobile in others, and AT&T in still others. If your whole setup depends on one SIM, you will hit dead zones that last entire days — not just minutes. That's fine for camping; it's a real problem on a work deadline.

A workable baseline has three parts: a primary hotspot with external antenna support, a secondary carrier or tethered phone as a backup, and clean cable routing that keeps signal paths intact. The antenna matters more than most guides admit. A hotspot device shoved in a cupholder gets a fraction of the signal it would get with an external antenna on a roof mount or window suction bracket. The hardware is inexpensive; the signal improvement is significant.

Power draw is worth planning around before you buy. A mobile hotspot plus a signal booster can pull 20–40W continuously. For a 700Wh system running eight work hours, that's 160–320Wh just for connectivity — meaningful load that needs to be budgeted alongside your laptop and fan, not discovered by accident mid-afternoon.

## Common Mistakes

Chasing theoretical max speeds is the most common waste of money in van internet setups. A hotspot advertised at 5G speeds rarely delivers anything close when the nearest tower is fifteen miles out. What actually matters is sustained connection reliability over an eight-hour workday. That's a different spec than peak throughput, and the two don't correlate cleanly.

Relying on one network is the mistake that disrupts real work. Deadlines don't care about dead zones. A second SIM from a different carrier costs $25–40 per month and has saved more remote work sessions than any single hardware upgrade. Treat the second carrier as insurance, not a luxury.

Ignoring power draw compounds quietly. Network gear pulling 25W for ten hours is 250Wh — roughly a third of a starter power station — before you've opened a laptop or run a fan. Internet infrastructure and power planning are connected decisions, not separate ones.

## What a Real Setup Looks Like

A budget-effective setup: one high-data plan from your primary carrier, one older phone or low-cost hotspot on a second carrier for backup, and a window or roof-mount antenna that connects to a hotspot dock inside. Monthly cost runs $60–120 depending on data needs. Power draw stays under 30W with low-draw hardware choices.

This isn't the fastest internet you've ever had. It's reliable enough to keep working when it matters — which is the actual goal.
