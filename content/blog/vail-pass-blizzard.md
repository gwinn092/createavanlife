We've had two genuinely frightening driving moments in 8 years of van life. Both of them were in Colorado, both of them involved ice and snow, and both of them happened because conditions turned faster than expected.

This is the first one.

## Vail Pass

Vail Pass on Interstate 70 is one of the most dangerous stretches of highway in Colorado during winter storms. It sits at over 10,600 feet, it runs through a gap in the mountains that funnels wind, and when a storm hits it hard, the transition from "manageable" to "everything is wrong" can happen faster than you can safely stop.

We hit it in a full blizzard.

I don't mean heavy snow. I mean a storm where the snowflakes are so large and so dense that when you turn on your headlights, the reflection off the snow immediately in front of the van blinds you to everything beyond it. We turned the headlights off. Driving in a blizzard at elevation with your headlights off because having them on makes visibility worse — that's the situation.

Around us, cars and trucks were sliding off the road. Not dramatically — slowly, helplessly, one after another pulling into guard rails or shoulders at angles that made clear they'd lost traction and hadn't fully gotten it back. The right side of the highway had a parade of vehicles that had made the same decision: get off before something worse happened.

## The Decision to Keep Moving

The correct decision in a bad weather situation is usually to pull over and wait. We've made that call many times. But on Vail Pass in that storm, stopping on the highway itself wasn't safe — the road was iced underneath the new snow, and pulling over meant risking being hit by another vehicle that couldn't stop. Moving slowly, staying in the tracks of the vehicle ahead, maintaining momentum — that was the less bad option.

There's a particular kind of fear in a situation where both stopping and continuing feel dangerous. You're making judgment calls in real time with limited information, in a vehicle that weighs several tons and is not behaving the way it normally does.

We eventually got to a spot where the grade leveled off enough to pull completely off the road — not onto the shoulder, but fully off into a pull-off area — and we waited. An hour, maybe more. When the plows came through, we followed them down the other side of the pass.

## What This Revealed About the ProMaster

A Ram ProMaster 2500 is front-wheel drive. On dry pavement, on wet road, in light snow — it's fine. On an iced mountain pass in a full blizzard, front-wheel drive is not ideal.

We got through it. But the experience made the 4x4 argument concrete in a way that specs and forum discussions never fully had. Not "4x4 would be nice" — "4x4 would have made that drive significantly less dangerous."

4x4 cargo vans cost $100,000 or more. That's not realistic for most people building a van life setup on a working budget, and we've accepted the tradeoff. But if your plan involves consistent mountain winter driving, that tradeoff deserves to be made consciously, not discovered on a pass at elevation in a blizzard.

## The Practical Lessons

**Know the road before you commit to it.** I-70 over Vail Pass in winter is a known hazard. We knew it. We underestimated how fast the storm was moving. Before any mountain pass in winter, check the conditions actively — CDOT's road conditions site, local weather, and recent traveler reports — not just the forecast.

**Winter tires are the minimum.** All-season tires on a loaded cargo van on ice at elevation are not an adequate safety system. Dedicated winter tires are worth the investment for anyone spending significant time in mountain winter conditions.

**Momentum management is everything on ice.** Braking causes slides. Acceleration causes slides. Smooth, slow, minimal inputs — that's the technique. And sometimes the right technique is to not be there at all.

**Have an honest conversation with your partner about risk tolerance.** My girlfriend and I have had direct conversations after each of these situations about what level of risk is acceptable and what conditions would make us park instead of continue. Those conversations, had in calm conditions, inform decisions made in not-calm conditions.
