Most parking garages, drive-throughs, and covered areas have a clearance limit of 9 feet. If your van is over 9 feet tall, you're excluded from a significant chunk of everyday infrastructure.

A stock Ram ProMaster 2500 (high roof) is approximately 9 feet 1 inch tall. Before modifications.

This is the measurement that shapes everything on the roof of your van — what fans you install, what racks you use, how you run your solar, and what you can never forget to do before pulling into a covered area.

## Why 9 Feet Matters in Daily Van Life

Drive-throughs are the obvious one. After a full day of driving or a long work session, you want coffee. If your van is 9 feet 2 inches, the franchise drive-through you've been to fifty times is no longer accessible without getting out and walking in.

Parking garages matter more than people expect. Urban van life — staying near cities, working near people, accessing gyms and co-working spaces — often involves parking structures. Most have clearance bars at 7 to 9 feet. Over 9 feet means street parking only, which is its own set of constraints.

Covered campground areas, maintenance bays, and car washes are in the same category. The 9-foot mark is where a lot of built infrastructure cuts you off.

## The ProMaster Roof Situation

A high-roof ProMaster stock is already right at the 9-foot line. Anything you add to the roof adds height. Roof racks, solar panels, vent fans — all of it goes up.

Standard roof racks can add 4–8 inches depending on design. A raised Maxxair fan adds roughly 4–5 inches above the closed position.

The Vantech low-profile ceiling rack I installed was chosen specifically because it keeps the ProMaster under 9 feet with everything loaded. It costs more than standard racks. The premium is worth it — the clearance headroom it buys back is worth more than the price difference.

## The Fan Rule Nobody Tells You

Here's the thing that matters most and gets mentioned least: **Maxxair fans, when raised (open position), push a high-roof ProMaster over 9 feet.**

The fans in the closed position may keep you under the line. The fans raised — as they would be during a drive with ventilation running — push you over it.

The rule: **lower your fans before pulling into any area with a clearance bar or low overhead structure.**

I have not hit a clearance bar. But I know people who have, and it's an expensive mistake — damage to the fan, the mount, sometimes the roof itself. The lesson is always "I forgot to put the fans down."

Make it a habit before it becomes a lesson.

## The Practical System

In our van, the pre-low-clearance checklist is two things:

1. Fans down
2. Nothing strapped to the rack that extends above the roofline

That's it. It takes ten seconds. It's the same category of automatic behavior as checking mirrors when changing lanes — you don't think about it, you just do it.

The habit forms fast if you make it conscious for the first month. Set a reminder if you need to. The cost of forgetting once is much higher than the cost of building the habit.

## What to Look for When Building

If you're in the planning stage:

**Measure your stock van height before buying anything for the roof.** Different trims, wheelbase options, and roof heights vary. Know your baseline.

**Add up the height of everything you plan to put on the roof.** Rack height (loaded) + fan height (open and closed) + panel thickness. Check that against your baseline and the 9-foot target.

**Low-profile racks are not just for aesthetics.** The Vantech low-profile design exists because the problem is real and van builders ask for it. The price difference versus a standard rack reflects real engineering, not just branding.

**The fans are the variable to watch.** Solar panels add fixed height. The fan is the variable — it changes height based on whether it's open or closed. Account for the open position in your maximum clearance calculation, and develop the habit of closing before any low-clearance entry.
