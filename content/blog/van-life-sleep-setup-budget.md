Sleep quality is the foundation of sustainable van life. If you are sleeping poorly — because of temperature, noise, light, or an uncomfortable surface — everything else in the build feels worse than it is. The good news is that a good van sleep setup does not require an expensive mattress or a complex platform build. It requires getting a few fundamentals right.

## The Sleeping Surface

### Foam Cut-to-Fit

A 4" to 6" layer of quality foam cut to fit your sleeping platform is the most cost-effective approach for most van builds.

**Cost:** A full-size piece of 4" high-density foam runs $80–150 depending on source. `Estimate` Foam supply stores (not mattress stores) usually have better prices and will cut to your exact dimensions. A sleeping platform built to a specific van width often requires a custom cut anyway — this makes foam the practical default.

**Firmness:** Higher density foam (2.5–4 lb per cubic foot) holds up better under nightly use. Cheap foam compresses and loses support within months. The middle-tier foam at most foam stores performs well for years.

**Ventilation underneath:** Foam on a solid surface without airflow traps moisture from body heat overnight. Slat-style platforms with gaps, or a piece of 1/4" plywood with holes drilled through it, allow air circulation that reduces moisture buildup under the foam. This matters more in humid climates.

### Platform Types

**Storage-first platforms:** Designed with drawers, lift-top access, or compartments underneath. More build complexity, higher upfront cost, but genuinely useful storage for seasonal gear, tools, and supplies.

**Ease-first platforms:** Simple box or slat construction with no storage access. Easier to build, easier to modify. Better choice if you are not sure yet how your storage needs will develop.

**Convertible platforms:** Fold-down designs that allow the rear of the van to open up for seating, cargo, or a table configuration. Higher complexity. Useful for people who want multi-use space. Often requires compromises in sleeping surface rigidity.

For most first builds, a simple fixed platform with thoughtful storage elsewhere is the easiest starting point. You can always rebuild once you know what you actually need.

## Temperature Management

Sleeping temperature is usually the thing that determines whether van life is comfortable or not — more than the mattress, more than the noise.

**Fan placement:** A roof fan on exhaust (pulling air out) creates low pressure that draws cooler air in from vents or cracked windows. For summer, this is more effective than almost anything else you can do. The difference between a properly ventilated van and a sealed one on a warm night is 10–20°F. `Estimate`

**Window cracking:** Even 1/2" of open window on the side opposite your roof fan creates crossflow. Without an intake path, a roof fan mostly recirculates interior air.

**Sleeping bag vs. blanket:** A sleeping bag rated to the temperature range you expect is more efficient than a blanket for cold weather. For warm weather, a light sheet works better than most blankets. Having both available beats trying to find a single middle-ground solution.

**Cold-weather specifics:** For temperatures below 45°F, a heated blanket or heated mattress pad (10–30W, runs from your power station) adds significant warmth without needing a full combustion heating system for mild cold. See the [heating post](/blog/van-life-heating-budget/) for serious cold weather.

## Blackout Curtains

Blackout curtains serve two purposes: sleep quality and stealth.

For sleep quality, even ambient streetlight or early sunrise can disrupt sleep significantly. A van without blackout coverage will wake you earlier than you want in summer and in urban parking.

For stealth, light showing through windows at night signals occupancy. If stealth matters in your parking situation, blackout coverage is not optional.

**Options:**
- Reflectix cut-to-fit in window frames: cheap ($20–40 for all windows), good light blockage, doubles as insulation. Looks obviously like a camper from outside when visible.
- Fabric curtains behind cab partition: better looking, requires sewing or curtain clips, less complete coverage.
- Commercial window covers (some brands make van-specific sets): $50–120 per window, good fit, easy to use. Cost adds up quickly for full coverage.

A combination approach — Reflectix for cargo area windows, fabric curtain between cab and cargo — works well at low cost.

## Noise Management

Van noise divides into two categories: the kind you can reduce and the kind you cannot.

**What you can reduce:** Road vibration resonance from loose panels and thin walls. Adding insulation and securing panels reduces rattles and dampens road noise meaningfully.

**What you mostly cannot control:** Traffic noise, neighbors, campground activity, rain on a metal roof. Earplugs cost $5 for a 50-pack and solve most of these problems. A white noise app on your phone covers variable noise patterns that earplugs alone do not fully mask.

The noise category that surprises most new van lifers is rain. A metal roof with no insulation sounds like a snare drum in a moderate rain. Ceiling insulation (particularly 1" or more of rigid foam plus a surface layer) absorbs a significant portion of impact noise from rain. This is worth doing during the build even if you are not in a rainy climate — you will eventually be.

## Before You Spend: A Checklist

Before buying any sleep-specific gear:

- Have you actually slept in the van without the upgrade? Many people solve "sleep problems" with gear that does not address the real cause.
- Is the problem temperature? Address ventilation and insulation before buying a more expensive mattress.
- Is the problem light? A $15 sleep mask solves the same problem as a $120 window cover system if you are the only one affected.
- Is the problem noise? Earplugs cost less than soundproofing and work immediately.
- Is the problem the platform height or stability? Recheck the build before replacing the foam.

The best sleep setup is the simplest one that works. Start with what you have, identify specifically what is not working, and address that specific problem. Buying ahead of identified problems is how van builds get expensive without getting better.
