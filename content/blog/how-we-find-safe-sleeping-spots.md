Finding safe places to sleep is the question new van lifers ask most often, and it's the one that's hardest to answer in a way that actually helps. The honest answer is: you develop a system over time. Here's ours.

## Year One vs. Year Eight

In year one, every night had a low-grade uncertainty that was hard to shake. We'd find a spot, park, and then spend the first hour doing quiet checks — is anyone else around, does this feel right, what's the exit situation if we need to move. Some nights were fine. Some nights we moved once or twice before we felt settled.

Eight years later, most nights are routine. We find a spot, we park, we go to sleep. The uncertainty hasn't gone away entirely — it's part of van life and probably always will be — but pattern recognition has built up to the point where the baseline is comfort, not vigilance.

The difference between year one and year eight isn't luck or a better van. It's accumulated knowledge about what good spots look and feel like.

## Our Actual Sources

**iOverlander, Freecampsites.net, and The Dyrt** are the apps we use most. These are community-sourced databases of camping and sleeping spots — van lifers, overlanders, and campers logging spots they've used with notes on conditions, cell signal, noise level, and whether they got asked to move.

The notes matter more than the location. A pin on a map tells you where. The comments tell you whether it's actually comfortable — whether it's quiet, whether locals care, whether it's a spot other people have used for years or one that's been shut down since the pin was added.

**Google Maps** for reading the surroundings. Satellite view of a potential spot tells you if there's a neighborhood nearby, what the road situation looks like, and whether there's nearby commercial activity that might generate early-morning noise or attention.

**Other van lifers.** Community knowledge — from forums, social media, in-person conversations at spots — fills gaps that apps don't. Someone who parked in a specific area last month knows things about it that a two-year-old app pin doesn't.

## What We Look For

**Residential quiet.** A quiet industrial area with no overnight activity is often better than a residential neighborhood, even though it feels counterintuitive. Residential neighbors notice unfamiliar vehicles. Industrial areas often have nobody around between 6 PM and 7 AM.

**Multiple exit options.** We don't like parking at a dead end. Not because we've ever needed to leave quickly, but because having options makes sleeping easier.

**No obvious reason for attention.** A spot that works is boring — nothing unusual about a van parked there overnight. Spots that feel wrong often have a reason: it's in front of someone's house, it's clearly private property that's not posted, it's a popular daytime area that empties at night in a way that feels conspicuous.

**Cell signal.** Not always possible, but good signal means better sleep. If something needs handling overnight or in the morning, connectivity matters.

## The System

Our pre-sleep routine in a new spot:

- Park and take five minutes to assess. Not a formal checklist — just awareness. What's around, who's around, does anything feel off.
- Check the apps for notes on that specific area if we haven't already.
- Lights out quickly. The longer you're visibly active, the more likely you attract attention. Get settled and dark.
- Have a plan for the "someone knocks" scenario. This has happened occasionally over 8 years. The response is the same: be friendly, be brief, if asked to move, move without drama. No situation in 8 years has escalated beyond a polite knock.

## What We'd Tell Year-One Us

The uncertainty gets smaller faster than you expect. The first month feels uncertain almost every night. By month six, most nights are routine. By year two, you have enough pattern recognition that you trust your read on a spot before you park.

The internet is your primary tool — other van lifers have been almost everywhere you're going, and their notes are invaluable. Use the apps, read the comments, and add your own when you find something worth sharing.

And accept that the uncertainty never fully goes away — it just becomes manageable background noise rather than active stress. Eight years in, an occasional uncertain night is part of the deal. It's also part of what makes the good spots feel earned.
