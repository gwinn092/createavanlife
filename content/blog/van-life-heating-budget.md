Cold weather van life is manageable. It requires planning, and it requires being honest about what different heating approaches can actually deliver. Here is a clear-eyed breakdown of the three main options and when each one makes sense.

## Option 1: Diesel or Propane Combustion Heater

Diesel air heaters (often sold under clone brand names at $100–200 or under established brands like Webasto or Espar at $600–1,500) are the most common full-time cold-weather solution. They burn diesel or propane to generate warm air that circulates through the van interior.

**When it works well:** Any sustained cold weather situation — temperatures well below freezing, multi-day mountain stays, extended winter seasons. Combustion heaters do not depend on your power station capacity. A diesel heater draws 10–30W to run its electronics and fan. `Spec` It can run all night on a fraction of a gallon of fuel.

**Clone vs. brand — honest tradeoffs:** Clone diesel heaters in the $100–200 range work for many people. They also have higher variability in build quality and less reliable customer support. A significant minority of buyers report early failures, fuel system issues, or glow plug problems within the first season. Brand-name units (Webasto, Espar, Autoterm) are more reliable but cost significantly more. Budget accordingly.

**CO and ventilation warning:** Any combustion heating device — diesel, propane, gasoline — produces carbon monoxide. CO is colorless and odorless. It will incapacitate and kill without warning. **Install a CO detector before running any combustion heater in a closed van. This is not optional.** Place it at sleeping level, not at ceiling height (CO mixes with air rather than rising). Test it before every cold-weather season. Replace batteries regularly.

Combustion heaters also require an exhaust outlet through the van body. The exhaust must exit completely to the outside. A poorly installed exhaust that recirculates even partially is a serious safety hazard. Follow installation instructions exactly, or hire someone with combustion heater experience.

**Ventilation reminder for combustion heaters:** Even with a properly installed exhaust, a small amount of CO infiltration is possible in certain parking situations (wind direction, enclosed spaces). Crack a window slightly when running a combustion heater overnight. A CO alarm should still be your primary protection.

**Cost range:** $100–200 for a clone unit plus install materials. $600–1,500+ for brand-name units.

### Propane Options

Small propane catalytic heaters like the Mr. Heater Buddy are popular for occasional cold nights. They are warm, portable, and require no installation.

**CO and ventilation warning:** Catalytic propane heaters produce CO, even though the amount is lower than open-flame heaters. **Use catalytic heaters only with a window cracked for ventilation, and run a CO detector whenever they are operating.** Never sleep with an unvented propane heater running.

Propane heaters are reasonable for brief cold nights and shoulder-season use. They are less practical for sustained winter living because propane canisters add up quickly.

## Option 2: Electric Heating from a Power Station

Electric heating — space heaters, electric blankets, heated mattress pads — run from your power station.

**When it works:** Mild cold (down to the high 30s Fahrenheit), short overnight sessions, and as supplemental heat on top of good insulation. Electric heating can handle the gap between "comfortable" and "still a bit chilly" without the complexity of a combustion system.

**When it drains faster than recovery:** A small 500W electric space heater running continuously overnight uses 4,000Wh — more than four times a typical 1kWh power station's capacity. `Spec` Even a 200W ceramic heater running all night exceeds most van power budgets. The math does not work for sustained cold weather unless you have a very large battery bank or reliable overnight charging.

Electric blankets and heated mattress pads are more efficient — they heat the sleeping surface directly rather than the air volume. A heated blanket running on low draws roughly 30–60W. `Spec` For mild cold nights, this is a practical option that does not destroy your power budget.

**Honest decision framework:** Electric heating works well as a comfort layer in mild cold when you have a healthy power station and reliable overnight recovery. It does not replace a dedicated combustion heater for serious cold weather van life.

## Option 3: Insulation-First Passive Strategy

The most underrated heating strategy is insulation. No insulation, no heat source works efficiently. Good insulation means your van holds heat from your body, cooking, and any supplemental source for much longer.

**What actually matters in a van:**

- **Floor:** Compression resistance matters more than R-value here. Rigid foam (polyiso, XPS) with a solid subfloor performs well.
- **Walls:** You are working in a thin profile. Thinsulate (1/4" to 3/8") is the most efficient option per inch. `Reported` Polyiso rigid foam cut to fit works at lower cost.
- **Ceiling:** The most important surface for heat loss in cold weather. Maximize R-value here. Two layers of 1" polyiso is a common budget approach.
- **Thermal bridges:** Metal van ribs and frame members conduct heat out. Covering these with insulation rather than letting them remain exposed makes a meaningful difference.

Condensation is the hidden problem with insulation in cold weather. Warm air from inside the van hits cold metal surfaces and deposits moisture. If moisture gets behind insulation and contacts bare metal, you get rust and mold over time. A small amount of intentional ventilation — a cracked window on the leeward side — allows moisture to escape rather than accumulate.

**The honest recommendation:** Before spending money on any heater, maximize your insulation. A well-insulated van with a $40 heated blanket can be comfortable in temperatures where a poorly insulated van with a diesel heater still feels cold. ## Decision Framework

Your climate and power situation should drive the choice:

- **Regular cold weather below 35°F:** Invest in a combustion heater. Insulate first. Run a CO detector. Never skip ventilation.
- **Mild cold weather (35–55°F), good power access:** Electric supplemental heat works. Heated blanket or low-draw ceramic heater. Insulation still matters.
- **Shoulder season / intermittent cold:** A catalytic propane heater with proper CO precautions handles occasional cold nights without permanent install.
- **Uncertain or mixed climate:** Start with insulation. Add a combustion heater when you have confirmed real cold-weather need and budget for it.

Safety first, always. Heat is not worth CO risk. Invest in a good CO alarm before any combustion heating device, and treat it as non-negotiable.
