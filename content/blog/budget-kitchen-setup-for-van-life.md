A practical kitchen cuts food costs and keeps road life sustainable.

## Core Setup

Two burners, one pan, one pot, and a sharp prep knife — that's the pattern that actually holds up over months on the road.

The reason to start small isn't frugality for its own sake; space is the real constraint. A two-burner stove fits on most fold-out surfaces and storage ledges without requiring a dedicated counter area. A single deep skillet handles stir-fries, pasta sauces, and scrambled eggs equally well. One good pot covers soups, rice, and boiling. When you add more cookware than that in the first month, it ends up wedged behind the seat or bungeed to something, and it never comes out.

Cleanup discipline matters more than most build guides mention. Simple setup means less surface area to wash with limited water. A complicated prep workflow isn't just inconvenient in a van — it's unsustainable. Dry pantry bins with consistent zones let you find ingredients without unpacking half the kitchen every meal. That kind of low-friction workflow is what actually makes cooking in a van enjoyable rather than exhausting.

## What to Skip at First

The single-purpose gadget trap is real and expensive. Rice cookers, mini waffle irons, sandwich presses — they solve specific problems, but not van life problems. They take up storage volume you don't have and only pay off if you use them several times a week. Most people don't know yet whether they cook rice three times a week. That's data you collect after living in a van for a month, not before. Buy after you know your habits, not based on what you think they'll be.

Large permanent installs deserve extra skepticism before you've completed a real trip. A built-in propane system or a full plumbed sink costs time, money, and wall space. If you discover two months in that you prefer eating out or using campground facilities, those installs can't be easily reversed. Start portable, test your actual cooking patterns, and make permanent decisions from real data.

## Power Considerations

Kitchen gear and power are connected decisions that new van lifers often plan separately. Electric induction cooktops pull 1,000–1,800W — more than most portable power stations can sustain comfortably. An electric kettle for pour-over coffee draws 1,000W for two minutes; that's manageable. A full induction dinner session is a different load profile entirely.

Propane or butane stoves sidestep this issue and are the default choice for good reason. If you want to cook electric, [understanding what your power system can actually run](/blog/how-many-watt-hours-do-i-need/) before buying appliances avoids real frustration. The kitchen and the power station are part of the same budget, and the honest math matters.
