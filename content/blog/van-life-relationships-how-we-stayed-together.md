The hardest thing about long-term van life isn't gear, or weather, or finding parking. It's being around one person 24 hours a day, every single day, with nowhere to go when you need space.

My girlfriend and I have been in a van together for 8 years. That's not a brag — it's context. Because the question we get most often isn't about power stations or solar panels. It's some version of "how do you not kill each other?"

The honest answer is that you work at it. Constantly. And some of that work is build decisions you make before you even move in.

## The Van Life Relationship Failure Pattern

Most van life relationship problems don't start with a fight. They start with friction that builds over weeks into something that feels much bigger than it is.

The van is small. Everything you do, you do together or in close proximity. You eat together, sleep together, work together, make decisions together — about where to drive, where to park, what to eat, when to leave. Decision fatigue is real. Being someone's only social contact is real. Having no physical separation when you need to decompress is real.

The couples who struggle are usually the ones who moved in without accounting for this. They built a beautiful van and assumed that because they loved each other, the rest would work itself out. Sometimes it does. More often, the friction accumulates until one big argument happens about something trivial — and what's actually happening is months of small frictions finding an exit.

## Build Decisions That Affect Relationship Health

This sounds strange to say out loud, but some of the most important relationship decisions in van life are made during the build phase.

**Design for separation within the space.** This means different areas for different activities. A sleeping area that's distinct from the work area. Seating that doesn't require both people to face the same direction all the time. Small physical differentiation in a small space makes a meaningful difference in whether you feel like you have any personal territory.

**Headphone access.** This sounds minor. It isn't. The ability to put on headphones and be in your own world for an hour — without it being a statement about the relationship — is real personal space in a van. Make sure you both have it and both use it without social cost.

**Individual charging setups.** You need to be able to use your devices independently, not compete for the same outlet at the same time. Small logistical things that create friction add up.

**Make the van functional enough that it doesn't create constant problems to solve together.** A van that's always breaking something, always too hot, always has a noisy rattle — these create a shared stress environment that puts the relationship under pressure that has nothing to do with the relationship.

## What We've Actually Learned

**You have to be able to disagree out loud.** In a house, you can leave the room, cool down, come back. In a van, you're both still there. Learning to disagree without it escalating — and to actually resolve things instead of letting them sit — is not optional. This is a skill. It can be learned. You work on it.

**Time apart matters even when you're in the same space.** A walk alone. An errand run solo. An hour with headphones on at the table while the other person does something else. Physical separation in a small van isn't always possible, but psychological separation — each person in their own world for a period — is necessary and possible.

**Not every place works for both of you equally.** After 8 years, we've figured out that some regions and environments suit one of us better than the other. Colorado summers work well for both. Florida is better for her in winter than for me. We negotiate around this, and we've gotten better at it — but we had to acknowledge that "what we want from van life" isn't always the same answer for both people.

**Respect the small things.** Van life amplifies small habits into big friction points. How someone leaves the kitchen. Noise when the other person is sleeping. The decision to change plans without discussing it. Things that wouldn't matter in a house become significant in a van. Acknowledging this — and actively managing the small things — is the daily maintenance work that keeps the big things from breaking.

## Why We're Still Here

Eight years is long enough that the answer isn't luck or chemistry, though those help. It's that we've built a life together where the friction gets addressed instead of accumulated, where both people's needs get negotiated instead of assumed, and where the thing that makes it hard — being together constantly — is also the thing that, most of the time, makes it worth it.

Van life is genuinely hard on relationships. It's also genuinely good for them if you approach it right. Eight years in, we're better at living together than we've ever been. Some of that would have happened anyway. A lot of it happened because the van left us no choice but to figure it out.
