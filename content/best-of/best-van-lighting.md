**Last reviewed:** March 2026

Lighting is one of the cheapest, highest-impact wins in a van build. A well-lit van feels larger, more livable, and more like a real home. An under-lit van feels like a storage unit you happen to sleep in. The good news is that effective van lighting costs very little and draws almost no power.

Do not overthink this. Here is what works.

## Wattage Reality Check

LED lighting is genuinely efficient. A typical van lighting setup — LED strips, a few puck lights, maybe a small overhead fixture — runs 10–20W total. `Spec`

For a 1kWh power station, 10W of lighting running six hours is 60Wh. That is 6% of your daily capacity. Lighting does not meaningfully compete with your fridge, laptop, or fan for power. It is a rounding error.

This means: do not spend energy worrying about which LED is 1W versus 2W. Focus on what looks right and what makes the space livable. The power math will take care of itself.

## Option 1: 12V LED Strips

**Wiring simplicity:** Moderate. A 12V LED strip connects directly to your van's 12V house circuit. This is simple electrical work — two wires, correct polarity, a fuse — but it does require touching the 12V system.

**Best for:** Full builds where you are already running a 12V system. LED strips mounted under cabinets, along the ceiling perimeter, or behind a valance create warm, even ambient lighting that feels more residential than a single overhead fixture.

**Dimming:** Most 12V LED strips can be paired with a $10–20 PWM dimmer. Dimming to 20–30% in the evening is a genuine quality-of-life upgrade — it changes the van from "bright workspace" to "cozy living space" without any rewiring.

**Color temperature:** 3000K (warm white) for living areas. This is the same warmth as an incandescent bulb. It feels calm and residential. `Spec` 4000K–5000K (cool white or daylight) for work surfaces and task areas where you need to see color accurately. Having two zones — warm ambient and cooler task — is the ideal setup even in a small van.

**Cost:** $15–30 for a quality 12V LED strip kit with connectors. `Estimate` Add a dimmer for $10–20.

## Option 2: USB-Powered Puck Lights

**Wiring simplicity:** None required. USB puck lights run from any USB outlet — power station USB port, USB car charger, or a simple USB hub. No 12V wiring at all.

**Best for:** Early builds where the 12V system is not yet installed, renters who cannot modify the vehicle, or anyone who wants to test lighting placement before committing.

**Performance:** Good for supplemental task lighting. Less effective for whole-van ambient lighting because the individual point sources are harder to diffuse.

**Cost:** $15–40 for a set of 4–6 puck lights with remote or touch controls. `Estimate`

**Honest limitation:** USB puck lights are a starting-point solution. They work, but over time most builders upgrade to a 12V strip system for better coverage, easier dimming, and cleaner cable routing.

## Option 3: Overhead Fixture

A single overhead LED flush-mount fixture gives broad ceiling coverage with minimal complexity.

**12V options:** Small 12V LED fixtures designed for RVs are widely available ($15–40) and connect directly to a 12V house circuit. They provide clean, even overhead light without visible strip runs.

**USB/battery options:** Battery-operated LED overhead fixtures (motion-sensor or touch-activated) require no wiring and work surprisingly well in small spaces. Battery cost over time is the main disadvantage.

**Best for:** Single-source lighting in a simple build, or as a supplement to strip lighting when you want a "on/off" main light.

## Color Temperature by Area

- **Sleeping/relaxing areas:** 2700K–3000K (warm white). Lower color temperature supports wind-down and feels less clinical.
- **Kitchen/prep area:** 3500K–4000K. Slightly cooler for food prep where you want to see what you are doing.
- **Work desk or reading area:** 4000K–5000K. Daylight-range temperature reduces eye strain during extended screen or close work.

In a van, you may only have one lighting zone. If so, 3000K is the right compromise — warm enough for evenings, bright enough for work.

## What to Skip

- **Colored accent lighting:** Fun initially. Rarely used after the first week. Save the money.
- **Complex smart-home lighting systems:** Not worth the complexity in a van-sized space. A $10 dimmer does everything you need.
- **High-output work lights as daily lighting:** A 500-lumen shop lamp creates harsh shadows and overlit spaces that feel uncomfortable for daily living.

## The Simple Rule

Start with one 12V LED strip under the ceiling perimeter or under your upper cabinets, with a dimmer. Add puck lights or task lighting where you identify gaps after a few weeks of actual use. That is a complete lighting system for under $60. `Estimate`

Van lighting is one of the cheapest wins available. Install it early in the build, adjust based on real use, and do not let it become a budget item that delays everything else.
