**Last reviewed:** March 2026

Insulation is the foundation of a livable van. Get it right and your heating and cooling systems work efficiently. Get it wrong and no amount of gear spending compensates for heat leaking through metal walls in winter or solar gain baking the interior in summer.

This page covers materials, placement priorities, and the decisions that matter most for budget builders.

## Do Not Use: Fiberglass Batts

Before the materials that work, the one that does not: fiberglass batt insulation designed for residential walls is not appropriate for van builds.

Fiberglass batts absorb and hold moisture. In a van, where moisture comes from breathing, cooking, and temperature differentials between the metal body and the interior air, held moisture leads to mold growth and accelerating rust on the metal body beneath the insulation. Even with a vapor barrier, fiberglass in a moving vehicle is difficult to seal adequately. This is a safety and durability issue, not a preference — skip it entirely.

## Material 1: Thinsulate (3M Thinsulate Acoustic Insulation)

Thinsulate is a synthetic fiber insulation that does not absorb moisture. Water passes through it rather than being retained. This makes it the most forgiving choice for van walls where condensation management is difficult.

**R-value:** Approximately R-1 per inch for the standard SM600L. `Reported` Thicker versions (SM1200L) offer approximately R-2 per inch. `Reported` These are lower than rigid foam alternatives, but the no-vapor-barrier advantage can outweigh the difference for many builders.

**Vapor barrier:** Not required with Thinsulate. This simplifies the install significantly and reduces the risk of trapping moisture behind the insulation layer.

**Best for:** Walls and ceiling. Thinsulate compresses easily around ribs and irregular surfaces that rigid foam panels cannot fill. The flexibility makes it practical for the compound curves of a van body.

**Cost:** Higher than polyiso per R-value. Expect $100–200 for enough material to cover walls and ceiling in a standard cargo van. `Estimate`

**Tradeoff:** Higher cost per R-value than polyiso. If budget is the binding constraint, polyiso and meticulous gap sealing can perform comparably.

## Material 2: Polyiso Rigid Foam Board

Polyiso (polyisocyanurate) foam board is the highest R-value per inch option in the rigid foam family. At approximately R-6 to R-6.5 per inch `Spec`, a single 1" panel provides meaningful thermal resistance in a thin profile.

**Best for:** Walls and ceiling where you have flat or nearly-flat surfaces, and floor where compression resistance matters. A 1/4" to 1/2" layer under the subfloor adds meaningful thermal break between the metal floor and the living surface.

**Vapor barrier:** Polyiso does not absorb water, but it is not vapor-permeable. Moisture that gets behind polyiso panels can accumulate. Sealing all edges thoroughly — with foil tape at seams and gaps — reduces this risk significantly. Any gap in the coverage becomes a thermal bridge and a potential moisture trap.

**Gap sealing:** This is the critical step with rigid foam. Gaps between the foam panels and the metal body, around ribs, and at corners allow condensation pathways. Fill gaps with spray foam sealant (not for volume insulation, just gap sealing).

**Cost:** $40–60 for a 4x8 sheet of 1" polyiso. `Estimate` Full wall and ceiling coverage in a cargo van typically requires 10–14 sheets.

## Material 3: Spray Foam (Gap Sealing Only)

Closed-cell spray foam as gap fill — the kind available in cans at hardware stores — is appropriate for filling cavities, edges, and areas where rigid panels cannot reach. It is not practical as a primary insulation material because of cost and difficulty controlling thickness.

Use it at:
- Seams between rigid foam panels
- Around roof rib edges and body panel joins
- The firewall between cab and cargo area
- Any irregular shape that rigid foam cannot fit

**Important:** Closed-cell spray foam against bare metal without a primer or rust-inhibiting layer can trap moisture against metal. In a new build on clean metal this is generally fine. On older vehicles with existing surface rust, prime the metal first.

## Floor vs. Walls vs. Ceiling: Different Priorities

**Floor:** Compression resistance matters most here. Thinsulate compresses under load and loses effectiveness. Rigid foam (polyiso or XPS) with a plywood subfloor above handles foot traffic and storage loads without compressing. A 1/2" to 3/4" layer under a 3/8" plywood subfloor is a common and effective approach.

**Walls:** Thin profile matters most. Walls are where you lose the most interior width if you go thick. Thinsulate (flexible, fills irregular surfaces) or 1" polyiso (highest R-value per inch) are both practical here. Cover all exposed metal ribs.

**Ceiling:** R-value matters most. Heat rises; in cold weather, ceiling insulation prevents the most heat loss. In hot weather, it reduces solar heat gain from the roof. Maximize thickness here — two layers of 1" polyiso is a common approach. `Reported`

## Vapor Barrier Guidance

If you use Thinsulate: no vapor barrier needed. This simplifies the install.

If you use rigid foam throughout: the foam itself acts as a moisture barrier on the warm side. Thorough edge sealing is more important than adding a separate barrier layer.

**Common mistake:** Installing a vapor barrier on both sides of insulation. This can trap moisture with nowhere to go. One side — or none with Thinsulate — is the right approach.

**Most important practice:** Ventilate intentionally. Even a well-insulated van produces moisture from breathing and cooking. A small cracked window for outward air movement prevents moisture from accumulating behind panels over time.

## Thermal Bridges

Van ribs and frame members are metal and conduct heat. If you run insulation panels between ribs but leave the ribs exposed to interior air, the ribs act as thermal bridges — conducting cold (or heat) from outside directly to your interior surfaces.

Covering ribs with insulation, not just the flat panels between them, makes a real difference in overall thermal performance. Thinsulate over rigid foam panel sections handles rib coverage naturally.

## Closing Recommendation

For most budget van builds:

- Use rigid foam (polyiso) as the primary wall and ceiling layer — it gives the best R-value per dollar.
- Fill every gap with spray foam sealant.
- Add Thinsulate over rigid panels if budget allows, particularly in the ceiling.
- Use compression-resistant rigid foam under the subfloor.
- Prioritize gap sealing over total material thickness — a complete 1" install beats a gapped 2" install.

Start simple, focus on coverage and gaps, and build permanently once you know your climate and use patterns.
